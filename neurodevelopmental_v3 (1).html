Before you start — how this guide thinks about brains

These are differences, not just deficits

Autism, ADHD, and dyslexia are real differences in how brains work — but the goal isn't to make a child "look normal." It's to help them thrive as themselves. Many strategies here help the child cope; the world also needs to adapt to them.

This guide is written from a neurodiversity-affirming perspective where possible, while still respecting that families dealing with daily struggles need practical help. Every tip is tagged with how strong the evidence is, and where there are competing views, you'll see them.

When to talk to a professional

Strategies in this guide can help — but they're not a substitute for proper assessment, especially if any of these are true:

  • The child shows signs of anxiety, depression, or self-harm
  • You suspect more than one condition (very common — see "Multiple" path)
  • School performance is clearly slipping despite effort
  • Strategies aren't working after 2–3 months of consistent trying
  • You want school accommodations (OKU card / Pendidikan Khas) which require diagnosis

Where to start in Malaysia: klinik kesihatan → paediatrician referral → child psychiatrist or child development clinic. Or private: paediatrician, child psychiatrist, or educational psychologist (RM 1,500–4,000 for full assessment). NGOs: NASOM (autism), Persatuan Dyslexia Malaysia.

What changed — an honest accounting

This guide has gone through three iterations. v1 had factual errors and gaps; v2 corrected them; v3 expands the scope significantly. Both critique passes were done out loud — here's the summary:

✨ New in v3 — additional subtypes & 5 related conditions

Within existing conditions, added:

  • Autism: Sensory-Dominant, Twice-Exceptional (2e), Autistic Burnout, Hyperlexia
  • ADHD: Sluggish Cognitive Tempo (SCT/CDS), Emotional Dysregulation/RSD, Twice-Exceptional ADHD
  • Dyslexia: Stealth/Compensated, Twice-Exceptional Dyslexia, Bilingual/Cross-Orthographic (Malaysia-relevant)

5 new top-level conditions added (each with subtypes):

  • Dysgraphia — writing-specific learning disability (Motor / Linguistic / Spatial)
  • Dyscalculia — maths-specific learning disability (Numerical / Procedural)
  • Dyspraxia / DCD — motor planning (Gross / Fine / Verbal)
  • DLD — Developmental Language Disorder (Receptive / Expressive / Mixed)
  • Tourette's & Tic Disorders — Provisional / Persistent / Tourette Syndrome

From v2 — corrections to v1 retained

Removed (not supported by evidence):

  • OpenDyslexic / dyslexia-friendly fonts — multiple controlled trials show no benefit
  • Coloured overlays for dyslexia — Cochrane reviews are largely negative
  • Vision therapy / behavioural optometry — AAP and ophthalmology bodies state explicitly it does not treat dyslexia
  • Magnocellular dyslexia subtype — not accepted as a clean subtype by current researchers
  • "Broken mirror" theory of autism — systematic reviews have rejected this

Caveated more honestly:

  • Stimulant medication — added that long-term outcomes (MTA 8/16-yr follow-up) are less clear than short-term gains
  • Orton-Gillingham — recent meta-analyses found no benefit over other structured-literacy approaches
  • Dyslexia subtypes — subtyping doesn't reliably guide treatment; most kids benefit from the same core approach
  • "30% executive function delay" in ADHD — Barkley's intuitive frame is contested

Added in v2 (still here): Masked/Internalised autism, PDA profile, "Show me everything" options, "More than one" comorbidity path, Clinician perspective, Evidence ratings on every tip, Neurodiversity-affirming framing, "Different views" callouts, "When to see a professional" prompts.

What's still missing

A Bahasa Malaysia version, reading-level toggle (Y3 vs Y6 language), a save/print/PDF export, a self-screening questionnaire, a printable handout for schools, deeper Malaysian cultural/religious framing, and a "this is contested" toggle to hide/show evidence-quality flags. These remain to-do.

Diagnostic pathway & resource directory

A practical map of how the Malaysian system works for neurodevelopmental concerns — what to do, in what order, where to go, and roughly what to expect. Built for parents in Malaysia; honest about the gaps.

The path, step by step

Every Malaysian family with a neurodivergent child broadly walks this path. You can start at step 1 or jump in mid-way — most families bounce between steps as new things come up.

1
Notice the signs

A teacher flags something, a relative notices a difference, your gut says "this isn't typical." Write down what you've observed (specific examples, when it happens, how often).

💡 Keep a 2-week diary before you see anyone — it's the most useful thing you can bring to a first appointment.
2
Talk to the school or kindergarten

Ask the class teacher and Guru Pemulihan (remedial teacher) what they're seeing. Some primary schools run early Pendidikan Khas screenings. The school can request observation by a counsellor or refer to LINUS / Pemulihan support.

💡 Schools rarely diagnose anything — that's not their role. But their written observations help the doctor later.
3
Visit a doctor — KK or private GP / paediatrician

Government route: Klinik Kesihatan (KK) → Family Medicine Specialist (FMS) → referral to specialist. Private route: walk-in or self-refer to a paediatrician. The doctor rules out medical issues (hearing, vision, thyroid, sleep) and decides whether a specialist referral is needed.

💡 Bring your diary, school feedback, and the child's growth/health book. Ask explicitly for a referral letter if you suspect autism, ADHD, or a learning disability.
4
Specialist referral & waitlist

Specialists who can diagnose: developmental paediatrician (~14 nationwide), child & adolescent psychiatrist (~26), general paediatrician with developmental experience, or a clinical psychologist registered with MSCP. In the government system, expect a 6–18 month wait for a specialist appointment in the Klang Valley.

💡 While waiting: start occupational therapy or speech therapy on your own — you don't need a diagnosis to begin therapy.
5
Formal assessment

A proper assessment usually takes multiple sessions: clinical interview, behaviour observation, standardised questionnaires (M-CHAT, Vanderbilt, Conners, ADOS), and sometimes a cognitive/literacy test. You'll receive a written report with diagnosis (if applicable) and recommendations.

💡 Make sure the report includes ICD or DSM-5 codes — these are needed for OKU registration and school accommodations.
6
OKU card & Pendidikan Khas placement

With the diagnostic report, register for an OKU card at JKM (optional but useful — opens up tax relief, allowances, school placement options). For school: choose between mainstream + accommodations, Program Pendidikan Khas Integrasi (PPKI) inside a regular school, or Sekolah Pendidikan Khas (SPK).

💡 OKU registration takes 1 week to 3 months. Make 3 copies of everything before you submit.
7
Therapy & ongoing support

Match therapy to the diagnosis: Speech-Language Therapy (SLT), Occupational Therapy (OT), Applied Behaviour Analysis (ABA), educational therapy for dyslexia, social-skills groups, family-based parent training. Review the IEP every 6–12 months. Re-assess if needs change at puberty.

💡 You're not signing up for a fixed plan — therapy needs change as the child grows. Stop what isn't working.

Government or private — the real trade-off

Most Malaysian families end up using both. Here's how they actually compare in 2026:

🏥 Government (KK / Hospital)
  • Cost: RM 1–5 nominal per visit; medication often free or heavily subsidised
  • Wait: 6–18 months for first specialist appointment in Klang Valley; faster in some states
  • Path: KK → MO → FMS → paeds/psych → developmental paeds (referral chain)
  • Strengths: Reports accepted everywhere; lifetime continuity; OKU pathway smoother
  • Weaknesses: Long waits; short appointments; limited therapy slots; geographic gaps
🏨 Private clinic / hospital
  • Cost: Paeds visit RM 150–450; full clinical-psych assessment RM 1,300–2,600; OT/SLT RM 100–200 / session
  • Wait: Often same week; choose your specialist; longer appointments
  • Path: Walk-in or GP referral → straight to paediatrician / clinical psychologist
  • Strengths: Speed, choice, depth; better for second opinions
  • Weaknesses: Expensive; quality varies; some reports not accepted by every government school — verify first

OKU registration — what to bring

Walk into JKM with all of this in hand and you'll usually finish the submission in one visit.

  • Form BPPOKU (Pindaan 2/2019) — download from jkm.gov.my, fill Bahagian A and C yourself
  • Medical sections completed — Bahagian D, E, F, G, H, I signed and stamped by an MMC-registered doctor or NSR-registered specialist
  • Diagnostic report(s) — original + photocopy; must include ICD/DSM-5 codes
  • Child's MyKid or birth certificate — original + 1 photocopy
  • Parent / guardian's MyKad — 1 photocopy of front and back
  • One passport-sized photo of the child — recent, on light background
  • 3 copies of the full submission for your own files — you'll need them later for school enrolment, JKM allowances, tax relief
  • Old OKU card (re-registration only, if registered before 2011)
⚠️ If you're not in the Klang Valley

Most named organisations above cluster in KL/Selangor. Families in Sabah, Sarawak, Kelantan, Terengganu, Perlis, and rural areas have substantially less access. Practical workarounds: ask your nearest government hospital about its "unit perkembangan kanak-kanak"; check whether NASOM has a centre in your state (Kota Kinabalu and Kuantan have one); Sarawak families: see KAA in the directory above; use telehealth — several Klang Valley clinical psychologists now offer online assessments. Specialist developmental paediatricians are scarce nationwide (only ~14 total) — a long wait does not mean your case is being ignored.
