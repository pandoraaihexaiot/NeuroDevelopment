Before you start — how this guide thinks about brains

These are differences, not just deficits

Autism, ADHD, and dyslexia are real differences in how brains work — but the goal isn't to make a child "look normal." It's to help them thrive as themselves. Many strategies here help the child cope; the world also needs to adapt to them.

This guide is written from a neurodiversity-affirming perspective where possible, while still respecting that families dealing with daily struggles need practical help. Every tip is tagged with how strong the evidence is, and where there are competing views, you'll see them.

When to talk to a professional

Strategies in this guide can help — but they're not a substitute for proper assessment, especially if any of these are true:

  • The child shows signs of anxiety, depression, or self-harm
  • You suspect more than one condition (very common — see "Multiple" path)
  • School performance is clearly slipping despite effort
  • Strategies aren't working after 2–3 months of consistent trying
  • You want school accommodations (OKU card / Pendidikan Khas) which require diagnosis

Where to start in Malaysia: klinik kesihatan → paediatrician referral → child psychiatrist or child development clinic. Or private: paediatrician, child psychiatrist, or educational psychologist (RM 1,500–4,000 for full assessment). NGOs: NASOM (autism), Persatuan Dyslexia Malaysia.

What changed — an honest accounting

This guide has gone through three iterations. v1 had factual errors and gaps; v2 corrected them; v3 expands the scope significantly. Both critique passes were done out loud — here's the summary:

✨ New in v3 — additional subtypes & 5 related conditions

Within existing conditions, added:

  • Autism: Sensory-Dominant, Twice-Exceptional (2e), Autistic Burnout, Hyperlexia
  • ADHD: Sluggish Cognitive Tempo (SCT/CDS), Emotional Dysregulation/RSD, Twice-Exceptional ADHD
  • Dyslexia: Stealth/Compensated, Twice-Exceptional Dyslexia, Bilingual/Cross-Orthographic (Malaysia-relevant)

5 new top-level conditions added (each with subtypes):

  • Dysgraphia — writing-specific learning disability (Motor / Linguistic / Spatial)
  • Dyscalculia — maths-specific learning disability (Numerical / Procedural)
  • Dyspraxia / DCD — motor planning (Gross / Fine / Verbal)
  • DLD — Developmental Language Disorder (Receptive / Expressive / Mixed)
  • Tourette's & Tic Disorders — Provisional / Persistent / Tourette Syndrome

From v2 — corrections to v1 retained

Removed (not supported by evidence):

  • OpenDyslexic / dyslexia-friendly fonts — multiple controlled trials show no benefit
  • Coloured overlays for dyslexia — Cochrane reviews are largely negative
  • Vision therapy / behavioural optometry — AAP and ophthalmology bodies state explicitly it does not treat dyslexia
  • Magnocellular dyslexia subtype — not accepted as a clean subtype by current researchers
  • "Broken mirror" theory of autism — systematic reviews have rejected this

Caveated more honestly:

  • Stimulant medication — added that long-term outcomes (MTA 8/16-yr follow-up) are less clear than short-term gains
  • Orton-Gillingham — recent meta-analyses found no benefit over other structured-literacy approaches
  • Dyslexia subtypes — subtyping doesn't reliably guide treatment; most kids benefit from the same core approach
  • "30% executive function delay" in ADHD — Barkley's intuitive frame is contested

Added in v2 (still here): Masked/Internalised autism, PDA profile, "Show me everything" options, "More than one" comorbidity path, Clinician perspective, Evidence ratings on every tip, Neurodiversity-affirming framing, "Different views" callouts, "When to see a professional" prompts.

What's still missing

A Bahasa Malaysia version, reading-level toggle (Y3 vs Y6 language), a save/print/PDF export, a self-screening questionnaire, a printable handout for schools, deeper Malaysian cultural/religious framing, and a "this is contested" toggle to hide/show evidence-quality flags. These remain to-do.
